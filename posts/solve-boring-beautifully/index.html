Stop Trying to Solve Big Problems. Solve Boring Ones Beautifully.

4 min · 689 words · Matt Livingston

Big problems are sexy. They get the headlines.
They sound ambitious. They make pitch decks look impressive.

But they’re also where indie creators go to die.

Why? Because big problems come with:

  • Big expectations
  • Big competition
  • Big complexity
  • Big burnout

You want to build something people use?
Stop trying to be impressive. Start solving boring things beautifully.

The Power of Boring Problems#

Boring problems are:

  • Repetitive
  • Annoying
  • Unremarkable
  • Underserved

In other words — they’re perfect.

Because they don’t attract VC money.
They don’t show up in trend reports.
They don’t trigger “wow, game-changer” reactions.

But they stick.
They get bookmarked.
They get used weekly.
And they build trust.

That’s the goal.

What Does “Beautiful” Mean?#

Let’s be clear: I’m not talking about visual design (though good design helps).
I mean the experience of using your tool should feel:

  • Instant
  • Effortless
  • Satisfying
  • Uncluttered

A boring problem solved beautifully is like a clean bathroom at a gas station: unexpected, appreciated, and remembered.

Example: The Copy Rewrite Problem#

Writing better sales copy is a boring, annoying, evergreen task.

It’s not “disruptive.” It’s not “AI for Healthcare.”

It’s just:

“I don’t like how this reads — help me fix it.”

So I built CopyKiller — a tool that makes weak copy bleed a little before rewriting it with punch.

It’s simple. It’s fast. It’s satisfying.

No signup. No dashboard. Just input → brutality → better output.

People love it because it handles a boring task with personality.

Example: The Task Overload Problem#

Everyone has a to-do list problem.
Too many items. Not enough clarity.

There are a thousand productivity apps. Most are bloated, gamified, or buried in UI fluff.

So I built Cutlist.

You paste in your tasks. It scores them ruthlessly and tells you what to delete.

It’s boring — until it’s brilliant. Because it works. Fast. With zero thinking.

And that’s beautiful.

What Makes a “Boring” Problem Worth Solving?#

Look for these signals:

✅ It’s Repetitive#

People do it weekly, daily, or monthly — but hate it every time.

✅ It’s Low-Stakes but High-Friction#

Nobody’s life depends on it. But it steals time and mental energy.

✅ It’s Ubiquitous#

Even if people don’t talk about it, everyone deals with it.

✅ It’s Hackable#

You can solve it in a few clicks or clever UX tricks.

How to Find Boring Gold#

  1. Look at your browser history
    What tool do you open out of obligation?

  2. Scan Reddit threads
    What tiny things do people complain about in your niche?

  3. Use your own friction
    What task makes you sigh before doing it?

  4. Find shadow workflows
    What are people doing with spreadsheets that could be turned into a tool?

Why Beautiful Beats Big#

Big problems attract pitch decks.
Beautiful tools attract users.

Big problems require scale.
Beautiful tools require taste.

Big problems raise funds.
Beautiful tools raise trust.

You’re not a VC-backed startup.
You’re a solo operator (or a small team) building microtools.

You don’t need a moonshot. You need a tool that someone uses 3 times a week.

That’s how leverage builds.

Build Boring? Cool. Now Make It Feel Good.#

Once you’ve identified your boring task, obsess over:

  • Fast load times
  • Smooth inputs
  • Delightful outputs
  • No friction

When someone solves a problem they didn’t want to deal with — and it felt good?
They tell others.

They come back.

They trust your brand.

And suddenly… your boring tool is a beloved shortcut.

Bonus: Boring Problems Are SEO Gold#

Because no one else is writing about them.

Try searching:

  • “best to-do list sorter with prioritization”
  • “rewrite copy to sound punchy but short”
  • “how to save time rewriting landing pages”

If your tool solves a boring problem and you write content around it, you win the long tail.

Boring + beautiful + searchable = growth.

Final Thought#

You don’t need to invent the next Stripe.

You need to make a tiny problem melt away in 10 seconds or less.

That’s the art. That’s the edge.

Solve boring beautifully. Then scale the leverage.
